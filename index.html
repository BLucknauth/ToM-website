Could it be possible that we have specialized neural systems for understanding others?

We hear it time and time again- “Humans are social creatures”. For much of human history the ability to survive has been intertwined with forming strong social connections. As a result of such extensive social behavior, it evolutionarily follows that specialized mechanisms to foster relationships would develop.

Theory of mind (or mentalizing) is the ability to understand others’ mental states. There are a multitude of explanations for how exactly we do this, but generally they fall into two categories: theory-theory and simulation theory.

Theory-Theory states that we have a set of rules and principles that determine behavior. These rules and principles are explicitly stored. Theory-theory is typically domain-specific, meaning it argues there is a specific system in the brain solely responsible for mentalizing. This view of the brain is controversial, as most social neuroscientists agree that there is a low likelihood of complex mental functions being completely localized.

On the other hand, Simulation Theory is a more domain general theory of mentalizing. Simulation theory states that we understand others’ mental states by simulating their mental states in our own minds. This can be through creating a model of the other person’s situation or the automatic activation of neural circuits corresponding to the other person’s situation. The latter is supported by the discovery of mirror neurons.
